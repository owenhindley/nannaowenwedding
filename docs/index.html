The Location

Þórsmörk is a region and valley in South Iceland, bordered on one side by Eyjafjallajökull (yep, that volcano from 2010), and the Icelandic highlands on the other. It's got a great big river running through the middle (Krossá), which gets fed by smaller rivers coming down off the glacier, some of which pass straight through the road.

The campground / cabin area in Þórsmörk we'll be in is called Básar, the largest and best-equipped in the valley. It's surrounded by dramatic rocky outcrops, cosy forests, tiny glacial streams and woody paths winding up to epic mountainside meadows where you might just catch sight of an arctic fox - or find some wild blueberries to snack on. In one direction you can see glaciers, or the red-orange mountains in Landmannalaugar, in the other direction you can almost see to the coast and the North Sea.

It's one of the most beautiful places in all of Iceland, and we're excited to welcome you all!

Facilities

Básar is the start and end point for two of the most popular hikes in Iceland. So they have exactly what you'd want after a day or four living out of a backpack and sleeping in a tent. Hot showers, electricity, 4G signal, toilets (with toilet paper), a small shop and even a bar. The shop sells hot dogs, grilled sandwiches, snacks and a limited amount of spare camping bits.

You won't be able to buy a new sleeping bag there, but they may sell you a pair of thick woolly socks when you discover that your H&M ankle socks aren't quite cutting it.

By Air

If you don't already live in Iceland, it's very likely you'll need to book a ticket on a plane to get from where you currently live to our wedding, on account of it being surrounded by sea, and quite a long way away from anything else.

Þósmörk does technically have its own airport (BITM), but you might struggle to find people to fly you there, and most importantly there is no duty-free on arrival (or actual buildings of any kind), so we recommend Keflavik International Airport (KEF) to be on the safe side.

Keflavik is a small, cosy airport, with a very well stocked duty-free (see Food & Drinks). If you look out of the right window when you're coming in from Europe, you might just see the latest volcano smoking away (if you're coming from the US, you're out of luck). There's also an airport in Reykjavik City, but that's only for domestic trips (with some exceptions).

From Keflavik Airport your options are:

  • Take a transfer bus to Reykjavik (45 minutes - we reccomend FlyBus), and depending on when you arrive, spend some time there before taking another bus to Þórsmörk Básar (options for public buses)
    - or -
  • Hire a regular car at the airport (recommendations), go for a sightseeing drive, and park the car near Seljalandsfoss and take a bus to Þórsmörk Básar (you can't drive a regular car the whole way due to river crossings)
    - or -
  • If you're experienced at 4x4 off-road driving, and are confident driving through rivers, you can hire a bigger car and drive the whole way to Básar yourself. There's one significant river crossing, to a depth of around 1m (depending on the weather). There's also 4x4 campervans available from (link).

We may be chartering / hiring buses to make the journey more straightforward (and fun!), we'll post more about this soon.

By Foot

Þórsmörk is the finishing point for two of Iceland's most popular hikes, Laugavegur (3-4 days) and Fimmvörðuháls (1 day).

Actually, Owen walked Fimmvörðuháls with Nanna on his very first trip to Iceland, which is the main reason why we wanted to hold the wedding here.

Some of you have already expressed an interest in hiking to the venue - and we can transport anything you don't need for the hike directly to the venue (like your tents, if you stay in the huts along the way).

Just message us if you're interested, and we'll tell you all you need to know, as well as connecting you with other people who want to do the same.

By Sea

You can travel by sea from Europe to Iceland - the Smyril Line ferry travels from Hirtshals in Denmark to beautiful Seyðisfjörður on the East coast of Iceland, taking 2-3 days, depending on the schedule (link)

It's a car ferry, so it's ideal if you're looking to make a bigger trip with a campervan or your own car. The ferry also stops off at the Faroe Islands along the way.

Once you land in Iceland, you'll then need to drive yourself to Seljalandsfoss (we'd reccomend taking at least two days to do this, there's plenty to see on the way), and then either transfer to a bus going to Þórsmörk Básar (you can't drive a regular car or van the whole way due to river crossings), or hire a 4x4 to drive the whole way if you're confident (see above).

There are public buses going that way, if you're feeling adventurous, from Egilsstaðir - see Straeto (Icelandic public bus system)

If you're taking the ferry away from Iceland, you can take the 'north way around' via Akureyri back to Seyðisfjörður, for which we'd reccommend spending at least 2-3 days.

Accommodation

Being a stunningly remote location in the Highlands, there are no 5-star hotels nearby. You'll have three main options for accomodation - a bed in the cabin, a campervan, or bringing your own tent.

The Cabins

Basar has two large wooden cabins, and sleeps 75 friendly people across two floors each.

As you can see from the photos, it's bunk-bed style sleeping, and you'll need to bring your own bedding (a sleeping bag or duvet / bedcovers). The cabins are both very warm and cosy, have power points, toilets, showers (with underfloor heating!) and kitchen which you'll have full acess to regardless of whether you're staying there or not.

We're getting a special deal on the cabins, two nights for 10,000 ISK per person.

Campervans

If you're an experienced off-road driver, and comfortable with river crossings (there's several, with the largest around 1m deep and 20m wide), you can rent a 4x4 campervan from (link).

Note that if there's been heavy rain, the river might be higher than usual, and you may have to take a bus - but we'll be travelling there ahead of time, so can let everyone know what the state of the road is.

The campsite charges 2000 ISK per person per night for campervans.

Tents

Tents are the simplest option, and camping in the woods at Þórsmörk is a great experience. You can find a nice secluded spot in the forest next to a river (to keep the beer cool), or camp in one of the large, open spaces with benches and public grills. The area is large, but easy to navigate.

The campsite charges 2000 ISK per person per night for tents.

Clothing

Dress code is : Imagine you're going to Glastonbury / Coachella in October in the UK.

Meaning you can absolutely dress up - the more fabulous or outrageous the better for our part - but bear in mind that it may get a little cold when the sun goes down (between 23.00 and 00.00, after which it starts to come back up again)

So ditch the high heels in favour of a nice pair of boots or comfortable shoes, and Gore-Tex beats Brogues any day of the week.

A suit, however, is still a suit if you're wearing a layer of thermals underneath (that's what Owen'll be wearing), and woollen jumpers with tights are a timeless Icelandic wedding look.

The key to dressing for Iceland is layers - take them off when the sun comes out and you're sunbathing on a grassy hill, put them on when it starts hailing. No joke, we've had both of these things within the space of 4 hours, in Básar, at this time of year.

Kids

As much as we love your kids, we're hoping to spend this time with you! We're therefore asking if it's possible to find a way for you to be kid-free at the weekend, so you can enjoy the weekend to the fullest!

We also do understand that many of you are travelling from abroad (some from very far), so it's not possible to leave your kids behind.

In that case, please message us - Básar is a large campground and popular with families, and Þórsmörk (well, Iceland in general) is a great environment for kids with wide open spaces and no poisounous plants or animals. The nature however can be dangerous (fast, deep, cold rivers and steep rock faces) so it's good to be prepared.

Clothing

A 3-season sleeping bag, pillow and an inflatable camping mattress is all you need, even if you're in a tent - again, think Glastonbury.

This also applies if you're staying in the hut (minus the mattress), but a light duvet and bedding also works.
